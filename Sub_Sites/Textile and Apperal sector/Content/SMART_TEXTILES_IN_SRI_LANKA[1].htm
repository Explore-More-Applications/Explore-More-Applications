What Are Smart Textiles?

Smart textiles are fabrics embedded with electronic components or functional finishes that enable them to sense, react to, or adapt to environmental stimuli such as temperature, movement, or sweat. They combine textile engineering, electronics, nanotechnology, and chemistry.

Key Players & Real-World Applications in Sri Lanka

1. MAS Holdings Twinery Innovation Hub

         Firefly: Safety-focused activewear with built-in LED lights for visibility.

         Nova: Smart bra that tracks heart rate and breathing patterns.

         Wearable heating jackets for cold climates using conductive fabrics.

2. Brandix InQube

         SenseMove: Smart athletic wear that records body motion for physiotherapy or sports performance.

         AdaptAce: Smart lingerie that adjusts fit in real-time based on body movement and posture.

3. SLINTEC (Sri Lanka Institute of Nanotechnology)

         Nano-fiber textiles with self-cleaning, temperature-regulating, and antibacterial properties.

         Conductive inks for embedding sensors and circuits into fabrics.

 

Eco-friendly dyes in the textile industry

What Are Eco-Friendly Dyes?

Eco-friendly dyes are colorants made from natural, biodegradable, or non-toxic sources that minimize environmental harm during textile production. Unlike conventional synthetic dyes that release toxic chemicals, heavy metals, and non-biodegradable substances, eco-dyes aim to:

         Reduce water pollution

         Protect ecosystems and workers

         Comply with international environmental standards

         Support sustainable branding and green certifications


 

Types of Eco-Friendly Dyes

1. Natural Dyes

         Extracted from plants, fruits, roots, wood waste, or insects.

         Examples:

o    Mahogany wood waste → reddish-brown dye

o    Tea waste → brown shades

o    Turmeric → yellow

o    Indigo → blue

         Biodegradable, low toxicity, and often safer for skin contact.

2. Bio-Based Synthetic Dyes

         Created using microorganisms (like bacteria or algae) engineered to produce pigments.

         Example: Arboblend dyes from biopolymers.

         Offer consistent color like synthetic dyes, but with lower environmental impact.

3. Low-Impact Synthetic Dyes

         Use less water, energy, and no heavy metals or formaldehyde.

         These are certified under standards like OEKO-TEX, GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard).

Examples in Sri Lanka

         Hayleys Fabric PLC: Working on natural dyes from biomass and tea waste

         Dynawash T-Hues: Producing garments using sustainable, herbal, and biodegradable dyes

         SLINTEC: Researching green textile chemistry for industry applications

 

 

Development of sustainable synthetic fibers from water sources

(e.g., Plastic Recycling in Oceans)

This concept refers to recovering plastic waste from oceans, rivers, and other water bodies and transforming it into synthetic textile fibers, like recycled polyester (rPET). Its a form of circular textile innovation where waste becomes wearable reducing pollution and supporting sustainability.

Real-World Initiatives in Sri Lanka

1. MAS Holdings Ocean Plastics Initiative

         Partnered with the Parley for the Oceans program.

         Converts marine plastic waste into yarn used in athletic wear (e.g., bras, leggings).

         Collaborates with fishermen to collect coastal waste.

2. Eco Spindles (Subsidiary of BPPL Holdings)

         Located in Horana, Sri Lanka.

         One of only two companies in Asia that manufacture polyester yarn directly from recycled PET bottles.

         Recycles over 1 billion PET bottles per year, including some collected from beaches and coastal areas.

 

Odor control finishes in sportswear

Odor control finishes in textiles are special chemical treatments applied to fabrics especially sportswear to reduce or prevent unpleasant body odors caused by sweat and bacteria. These finishes help keep clothes fresher for longer, even after intense physical activity.

This is particularly important in tropical countries like Sri Lanka, where high humidity and heat promote bacterial growth and sweat-related odors.

1. MAS Holdings Odor Management Technology

         MAS Active (a division of MAS Holdings) integrates anti-odor and anti-microbial finishes in their sportswear, especially for brands like Nike, Decathlon, and lululemon.

         Brands under MAS often use silver ion or zinc-based finishes, which are chemically engineered to stop bacteria growth.

2. Hela Clothing & Brandix

         Sri Lankan manufacturers supply sportswear to global brands that demand performance features, including odor control.

         Some use natural-based odor control technologies (e.g., from coconut charcoal or plant extracts) in their product lines.

 

Bioengineered Living Fabrics: The Future of Sustainable Textiles

What Are Bioengineered Living Fabrics?

Bioengineered living fabrics are a cutting-edge innovation in textile science, made using living organisms like bacteria, fungi, or algae. These organisms are genetically engineered or cultivated to create or enhance fabric properties, making the textiles interactive, responsive, and sustainable.

Unlike traditional synthetic materials, these fabrics are designed to grow, self-repair, and even interact with the environment like absorbing carbon dioxide or reacting to temperature changes.

Key Features and Mechanisms

1. Self-Healing Properties

         These fabrics can repair minor damages like cuts or tears automatically.

         This is achieved using:

o    Smart polymers that reseal when triggered by heat or moisture.

o    Engineered microbes that regrow fiber or produce glue-like substances.

         Example: A tear in the fabric reseals independently without sewing, extending garment lifespan and reducing waste.

2. Carbon Dioxide Absorption

         Some living fabrics are engineered with photosynthetic organisms like algae that absorb CO₂ during respiration.

         Benefits:

o    Reduces atmospheric carbon, helping combat climate change.

o    Since the fabric surface area is small, it doesnt affect the wearer CO₂ is absorbed in tiny amounts and only from the surrounding air.

3. Environmental Responsiveness

         These fabrics can react to temperature, humidity, or sweat, adjusting properties like breathability or shape.

         Ideal for sportswear, medical clothing, or adaptive fashion.

4. Biodegradability

         Made from natural organisms, these textiles are biodegradable, reducing textile waste and microplastic pollution.

 

4D Textiles: The Smart Fabrics of the Future

What Are 4D Textiles?

4D textiles are advanced smart materials that change shape, structure, or function over time in response to external stimuli like temperature, moisture, light, or pressure.
The fourth dimension refers to time, meaning these textiles are not static but can evolve their form or performance dynamically.

They are made by combining programmable materials (like shape-memory polymers or hydrogels) with textile engineering, often using 3D printing, nanotechnology, or responsive coatings.

How Do They Work?

4D textiles integrate:

         Shape-memory materials that return to a predefined shape when triggered.

         Hydrogels that expand or shrink with humidity or pH.

         Thermo-responsive polymers that respond to heat.

         Photochromic or electroactive materials that react to light or electricity.

When exposed to the trigger (like body heat or sweat), the fabric will reconfigure its shape, ventilation, or texture automatically.

Key Features and Applications

1. Thermal Regulation

         Fabric opens micro-vents when the body is hot and closes them when cold.

         Ideal for sportswear, uniforms, or outdoor gear.

2. Medical Textiles

         Bandages or braces that tighten around injured muscles.

         Compression garments that adapt to swelling.

3. Adaptive Sportswear

         Changes structure during exercise for better support.

         Reduces drag or enhances comfort depending on movement.

4. Shape-Shifting Fashion

         Clothes that change style, length, or design based on temperature or lighting.

         For example, a jacket sleeve that shortens in hot weather.
