Phosphate Minerals (Eppawela)

  • Background: The Eppawela phosphate deposit in Sri Lanka is one of the largest in South Asia, estimated at over 60 million tons. Phosphate is a critical component of fertilizers, essential for agriculture, which employs over 25% of Sri Lankas workforce and contributes ~7% to GDP (as of recent economic reports).
  • Global Context: Globally, countries like Morocco dominate phosphate production (over 70% of global reserves). Sri Lankas local production can reduce dependency on such imports, especially amidst global supply chain disruptions (e.g., post-COVID or geopolitical tensions).
  • Chemical Details: The reaction to produce single superphosphate (SSP) is exothermic, requiring careful temperature control to avoid side reactions. Chemists also monitor the purity of sulfuric acid to ensure high-quality fertilizer output.
  • Economic Potential: Beyond fertilizers, phosphate can be used in food additives (e.g., phosphoric acid in soft drinks) or detergents, opening new revenue streams if processed locally.
  • Applications- mainly fertilizers, food additves, animal feed supplements, detergents SL Products of SSP

High Grade Eppawala Rock Phosphate (HERP):

  • Produced by Lanka Phosphate Limited (LPL), HERP contains 38% phosphorus and is marketed as a locally manufactured, cost-effective phosphate fertilizer suitable for long-term crops like paddy, vegetables, and fruits.

 Eppawala Rock Phosphate (ERP):

  • Also produced by LPL, ERP is another phosphate fertilizer variant from the Eppawala deposit, with varying phosphorus content (e.g., 28%, 32%, 38% as noted in some sources). Its used for agricultural applications, particularly for rice and other crops.

 Triple Super Phosphate (TSP):

Distributed by companies like Colombo Commercial Company, TSP is a concentrated phosphate fertilizer imported and sold in Sri Lanka for various crops

 

 

 

Mineral Sands (Ilmenite)

  • Background: Sri Lankas mineral sands, especially along the coasts of Pulmoddai, are rich in ilmenite, rutile, and zircon. The Pulmoddai deposit, managed by Lanka Mineral Sands Ltd., is a key exporter, contributing ~$15 million annually to Sri Lankas economy.
  • Global Context: Titanium dioxide (TiO₂) is a high-demand material globally, used in paints, cosmetics (e.g., sunscreens), and even food (E171, though controversial due to health concerns). China and the U.S. are major producers, but Sri Lanka can compete by focusing on nano-TiO₂ for advanced applications like photocatalysis.
  • Chemical Details: The alternative low-temperature synthesis of nano-TiO₂ using diluted HCl is a greener approach, reducing energy costs and emissions compared to traditional high-temperature methods. Nano-TiO₂ has a higher surface area, making it more effective in applications like solar cells.
  • Applications Expansion: TiO₂ is also used in self-cleaning surfaces (e.g., tiles that break down dirt under UV light), a growing market in sustainable architecture.

Gemstones (Ratnapura and Other Regions)
Background: Sri Lanka, historically known as Ratna-Dweepa (Gem Island), has a gem industry spanning over 2,500 years. It is among the worlds top five gem-bearing nations, with deposits in Ratnapura, Elahera, and Okkampitiya. The country produces over 75 varieties of gemstones, including sapphires, rubies, and spinels, from an estimated 25% of its land area that is potentially gem-bearing. The industry contributes significantly to exports, generating approximately US$400 million annually.

Global Context: Sri Lanka is a leading producer of high-quality sapphires (blue, pink, yellow, and rare Padparadscha) and the ninth-largest exporter of precious stones globally. It competes with countries like Myanmar and Madagascar but is renowned for its unique gem quality and ethical mining practices, with no child labor or exploitation.

Chemical Details: Chemists enhance gem value through processes like heat treatment, which involves controlled heating (e.g., 1,2001,600C) to improve color and clarity by altering crystal lattice defects in corundum (Al₂O₃, for sapphires and rubies). For example, heat treatment can transform pale sapphires into vibrant blue or pink stones, increasing their market value from $1,000$5,000/carat to over $10,000/carat. Chemical analysis using spectroscopy ensures gem authenticity and detects treatments, maintaining market trust.

Economic Potential: By processing gems locally (e.g., cutting, polishing, and heat treatment), Sri Lanka can increase export value significantly. For instance, a 13-carat untreated Ceylon sapphire fetched 243,200 at auction, showcasing high-value potential. Chemists also explore rare-earth element recovery from gem deposits for applications in electronics, adding new revenue streams. The industry supports a thriving domestic jewelry market, distinct from Western markets.

Applications: Jewelry (high-value sapphires, rubies), laser optics (using corundums durability), rare-earth element recovery for electronics, and collectibles (e.g., the 392.52-carat Blue Belle of Asia or 310-kg corundum sapphire).

SL Products: Polished sapphires (e.g., Padparadscha, blue sapphire), rubies, and custom jewelry from companies like Prestige Gems and local Ratnapura markets.
