01. Chemists in Agriculture: Enhancing Productivity and Sustainability

 

Existing Contributions

 

A. Fertilizer Optimization & Soil Science

  • Chemists developed Integrated Plant Nutrition Systems (IPNS), balancing organic and inorganic inputs to maximize yields and reduce chemical dependency.
  • Departments like Agricultural Chemistry at Eastern University focus on soil fertility, pollution control, and sustainable land use.

B. Policy Advocacy

  • During the 2021 fertilizer ban, chemists like Dr. Saman Dharmakeerthi led evidence-based responses, warning of risks to tea and rice production and preventing deeper economic setbacks.

C. Agrochemical Alternatives

  • Chemists researched glyphosate alternatives post-ban, though abrupt shifts led to productivity losses of 1832% in major crops, underscoring the need for gradual, research-based transitions.

 

Novel Innovations

 

1. Biofilm Biofertilizer

  • Developed by NIFS and Lanka Biofertilizers Ltd.
  • Uses microbial biofilms (e.g., Azotobacter, Rhizobium) to fix nitrogen and solubilize phosphorus more efficiently than free-living microbes.
  • Chemists optimize the biofilm matrix for stress tolerance and root adherence, improving soil microbial activity and plant growth.

Economic Impact:

  • Supports organic farming, boosts crop yield, and reduces import dependency on synthetic fertilizers.
  • Future work includes nanoparticle-enhanced biofilms and region-specific formulations.

 

2. Leaf Color Index (LCI) Technology

  • Chemists helped design precision agriculture tools to assess nitrogen status, reducing fertilizer waste by 2030% and improving input efficiency.

 

3. Waste-to-Fertilizer Conversion

  • Chemists lead urban waste and fecal sludge composting initiatives to produce organic fertilizers a potential solution to fertilizer shortages.

 

4. Protected Agriculture Systems

  • Collaborated with FAO to develop rain shelters and polytunnels, reducing pesticide use by 40% and doubling seed yields.

 

02. Chemists in Post-Harvest Technology: Reducing Losses & Supporting Exports

 

1. FruitCare Edible Coating

  • Developed by the National Institute of Post-Harvest Management.
  • Formulated by chemists using chitosan, glycerol, citric acid, and essential oils to create a biodegradable coating that:
    • Reduces spoilage, microbial decay, and moisture loss
    • Slows ripening and extends shelf life
    • Preserves quality for export markets

2. Chemist-Driven Future Enhancements:

  • Integration with smart packaging (e.g., ripeness indicators)
  • Expansion into cut-fruit and vegetable markets

 

 

03. Chemists and Eco-Friendly Pest Control: Supporting Safer Agriculture

 

LakGro Biopesticide

  • Protein bait targeting Bactrocera fruit flies, developed by Lanka Agroartis and DOA.
  • Chemists design formulations with target specificity, residue-free composition, and compatibility with organic farming.

Economic Value:

  • Reduces dependence on synthetic pesticides
  • Enhances fruit and vegetable marketability
  • Promotes GAP (Good Agricultural Practices) adoption

Ongoing Innovations by Chemists:

  • Weather-resistant, slow-release bait formulations
  • Expansion to other pest species

Coconut Oil Industry

. Quality Control and Chemical Analysis

  • Background: Sri Lanka is a major coconut producer, with over 2.5 billion nuts annually, contributing ~2% to GDP. Coconut oil is a flagship export, valued at ~$200 million yearly.
  • Global Context: Coconut oil competes with palm oil and olive oil in global markets. Its high medium-chain fatty acid (MCFA) content, like lauric acid (50%), makes it popular for health and cosmetic uses.
  • Chemical Details: Aflatoxin, a toxin from mold (Aspergillus species), is a major concern in tropical climates like Sri Lankas. Chemists use techniques like high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) to detect aflatoxin levels, ensuring theyre below the international limit of 20 ppb.

2. RBD Process (Refined, Bleached, Deodorized)

  • Background: The RBD process was developed to meet global standards for edible oils, removing impurities and extending shelf life. In Sri Lanka, its widely adopted by major producers like Cargills and Munchee.
  • Global Context: RBD coconut oil is preferred in markets like the EU and U.S., where consumers demand neutral-flavored oils for cooking and baking. Sri Lanka exports ~60,000 tons of coconut oil annually, much of it RBD.
  • Chemical Details: Refining removes free fatty acids via neutralization (using sodium hydroxide), bleaching uses activated clay to remove pigments, and deodorizing uses steam distillation to eliminate odors.
  • Sustainability: The process avoids chemical preservatives, aligning with global trends toward natural products. However, bleaching clay disposal requires careful management to avoid environmental harm.

Tea Industry

Ceylon Tea & Chemistry: Powering Sri Lankas Economic Growth

 

Why Ceylon Tea Matters

Ceylon Tea is one of Sri Lankas most valuable export commodities, earning over USD 1.43 billion in 2024, with targets exceeding USD 2 billion by 2025Ceylon Tea + Pharmaceut.

The industry supports hundreds of thousands of jobs across agriculture, processing, packaging, branding, and export.

Sri Lanka is shifting from a bulk tea exporter to a premium, value-added brand, with increasing global demand for teas health benefits and quality.

 

How Chemists Add Economic Value

Chemists are crucial at every stage of the tea value chain:

 

Quality Control

Ensure consistent polyphenol content and safety from pesticide residues.

Conduct microbiological and heavy metal testing for export standards (e.g., EU, Japan)

Product Innovation

Develop instant teas, tea-based nutraceuticals, and cosmetics using extracted tea catechins.

Partner with companies like Dilmah, Mlesna, and Stassen to create new health-driven tea lines.

Health Validation Research

TRI and University of Colombo validate teas antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and cardioprotective effectsvital for global marketing as a health beverage.

Packaging & Shelf-Life Chemistry

Help design biodegradable, oxygen-barrier packaging to preserve freshness and meet global sustainability standards.

 

Future Green Approach: Sustainable Tea Chemistry

 

Green Extraction Technologies:

Replace traditional solvent-based methods with supercritical CO₂ or ultrasound-assisted extraction to isolate tea compounds without chemical waste.

Zero-Waste Tea Industry Vision:

Tea Waste ➜ Cosmetics: Catechin-rich tea dust used in soaps, lotions, and facial scrubs.

Spent Leaves ➜ Fertilizer: Compost spent leaves to return nutrients to plantations.

Natural Dyes: Thearubigins and theaflavins used for textile and packaging coloring.

Sustainable Packaging Innovations:

Chemists can lead the use of bioplastics (from starch or tea cellulose) for compostable tea bags and wrappers, minimizing microplastic pollution.
